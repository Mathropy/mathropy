Ed chose two distinct natural numbers, $X$ and $Y$. ($X>Y$)  
He calculated four values, $S=X+Y, D=X-Y, P=X\times Y, Q=X/Y$.  
He randomly shuffled them and assigned one to each of his friends: **Alice, Bob, Charlie, David**.  
Each friend received exactly one value, and no two friends received the same number.  However, they didn't know which category ($S, D, P,$ or $Q$) their number belonged to.

(âš ď¸Ź **Note**: Initially, the values of $S, D, P,$ or $Q$ could have been identical. For example, if $X=4$ and $Y=2$, then $D=2$ and $Q=2$. In this case, Alice could receive $D$ and Bob could receive $Q$; they hold different "categories," but the numerical value (2) is the same.)  

Ed: If you know which of $S, D, P,$ or $Q$ you received, raise your hand!  
But nobody raised their hand.  

Ed: OK. Here is a hint. $S, D, P,$ and $Q$ are all distinct!  
Alice: Oh, then I now know which one I received.  
Bob: Really? If Alice knows that, then I know what $X$ and $Y$ are.  
Charlie: Wow, since Bob knows it, I know $X$ and $Y$ too!  
David: But I still don't know $X$ and $Y$.

Question. **What is the value that David received?**

It is variation of sum and product puzzle(https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sum_and_Product_Puzzle). I came up with the $S, D, P, Q$ setup first, and then thought about how to craft a good puzzle around it. And this puzzle is the result! I think Framing is really cool. Ed speaks first and gives a hint, then Alice, Bob, Charlie, and David each speak exactly once! This is my favorite puzzle :)

<details>
  <summary>Answer & Solution (Click!)</summary>
  The Answer is 18.
  
  First, consider Alice's perspective. We know that if $Q$ is not an integer, the person who received $Q$ would immediately know they hold $Q$. Since no one raised their hand initially, $Q$ must be an integer.<br>
  Since $S, D, P,$ and $Q$ are all distinct, $Y\neq 1$. Then $S\geq 5$, $D\geq 1$, $P\geq 6$, $Q\geq 2$. If $D=1$, $Q$ cannot be an integer. If $D=2$, $(X, Y)=(4, 2)$ (which means $D=Q=2$) or $Q$ is not an integer. So $D\geq 3$. This leaves $Q=2$ as the only value that uniquely identifies its category. Thus, Alice must have received $Q=2$.<br>
  Let $X=2Y$. Then $D\geq 3$, $Y\geq 3$, $S=3Y$, $D=Y$, $P=2Y^2$. Since Bob figures out $X$ and $Y$ just by knowing Alice holds $Q=2$, Bob must have received $D$. (If he had received $S$ or $P$, there might be ambiguities).<br>
  Because David still doesn't know $X$ and $Y$, it means David cannot distinguish whether his value is $S$ or $P$. This overlap happens when $S=P$ for different values of $Y$ (i.e. $3Y=2(Y')^2$), meaning David received a value of the form $18k^2$. If $k\geq 2$, it would contradict the fact that Bob could uniquely determine $X$ and $Y$. Therefore, $k=1$, and David received 18.
</details>
