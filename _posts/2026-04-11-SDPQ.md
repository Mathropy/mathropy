Ed chose two distinct natural numbers, $X$ and $Y$. ($X>Y$)  
And he calculated four values, $S=X+Y, D=X-Y, P=X\times Y, Q=X/Y$.  
He randomly swapped them... and assigned these to his friends, **Alice, Bob, Charlie, David**.  
Each friend received exactly one value of the four values, with no two friends receiving the same one.
And they didn't know whether it represented $S, D, P,$ or $Q$.

(⚠️ **Caution**: Initially, the values of $S, D, P,$ or $Q$ could have been identical. For example, if $X=4$ and $Y=2$, then $D=2$ and $Q=2$. In this case, Alice could receive $D$ and Bob could receive $Q$; they hold different "categories," but the numerical value (2) is the same.)  

Ed: If you know which of $S, D, P,$ or $Q$ you received, raise your hand!  
But nobody raises their hand.  

Ed: OK. Here is a hint. $S, D, P,$ and $Q$ are all distinct!    
Alice: Oh, then I now know whether my value is $S, D, P,$ or $Q$.   
Bob: Really? If Alice knows that, then I know what $X$ and $Y$ are.  
Charlie: Wow, since Bob knows it, I know $X$ and $Y$ too!    
David: But I still don't know $X$ and $Y$.    

Question. **What is the value that David received?**

<details>
  <summary>Hint!</summary>
  Consider Alice's perspective first. Why was she unable to raise her hand at first? Why does she know whether her value is $S, D, P,$ or $Q$ now?
</details>

(Background & Motivation)
It is variation of sum and product puzzle(https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sum_and_Product_Puzzle). I made setting of $S, D, P,$ or $Q$ first, and think about how to make good puzzle. And made this puzzle! Frame of puzzle is so cool. Ed said first, and gave hint, and Alice, Bob, Charlie, David said only once! This is my favorite puzzle :)
YES! It's FUN!

<details>
  <summary>Answer & Solution</summary>
  The Answer is 18.
  
  First, consider Alice's perspective. We know that if $Q$ is not an integer, the someone received $Q$ should know it is $Q$. So $Q$ is an integer. Since $S, D, P,$ and $Q$ are all distinct, $Y\neq 1$. Then $S\geq 5, D\geq 1, P\geq 6, Q\geq 2$. But if $D=1$, $Q$ is not an integer. And if $D=2$, $(X, Y)=(4, 2)$ (so $D=Q=2$) or $Q$ is not an integer. So $D\geq 3$. Then '$Q=2$ and Alice received $Q$' is only possible case.

  Let $X=2Y$. Then $Y\geq 3, S=3Y, D=Y, P=2Y^2$. Since Bob knew $X$ and $Y$, we know that Bob received $D$ and $D$ is not a type of $3Y(Y\geq 3)$ or $2Y^2(Y\geq 3)$. But David didn't know $X, Y$, it means David can't divide $S$ and $P$, so David received type of $18k^2$. But if $K\geq 2$, contradiction happens with Bob knew $X, Y$. So $k=1$, David received 18.
</details>
